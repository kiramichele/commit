What you're doing

Before you learn what an algorithm is formally, you're going to find one in the wild. Open a recommendation feed you use every day, look at what it's showing you, and try to figure out why. Form your hypotheses first — then we'll show you what these systems actually track.

Step 5 of 5 — the reveal

What these systems actually track

Here's what major recommendation platforms actually use — documented from their own published research, patent filings, and engineering blog posts. See how your hypothesis held up.

Show me what they actually track
Based on published research from YouTube, Spotify, Netflix, and TikTok engineering teams
Watch/listen timeThe single strongest signal on most platforms. A 90% completion rate outweighs a like. Skipping at 10 seconds is a strong negative signal.
Implicit feedbackRewinding, pausing, replaying, sharing, screenshotting — all tracked. You don't have to click anything for the system to learn from your behavior.
Collaborative filteringUsers who watched X also watched Y. You're being grouped with people who behave like you — you never consented to being in that group.
Session contextWhat you watched in this session, in what order. A horror movie followed by a comedy sends a different signal than the same two in reverse order.
Time and deviceLate-night mobile vs. weekend TV vs. Monday morning commute. Platforms serve different content at different times deliberately.
Content embeddingsMachine learning models that represent content as vectors — so if you like one thing, the model finds mathematically similar content even if you've never searched for it.
Business objectivesRecommendations are not purely about what you'll enjoy — they're also about watch time, ad revenue, subscriptions, and platform retention. These goals sometimes conflict with each other.
Cold start problemNew users get generic popular content until the system has enough signal. New creators get suppressed until engagement data builds up. The algorithm has a bias toward the already-popular.
The key takeaway These systems don't ask you what you want. They observe what you do — every pause, every skip, every rewatch — and build a model of you from that behavior. The recommendations you see are the output of that model. You didn't program it. You didn't consent to it. You just used the app.
What this connects to

What you just reverse-engineered is an algorithm making decisions about you using data you generated without thinking about it. This is the foundation of everything we'll study in Unit 2 (Data), Unit 3 (Algorithms), and Unit 5 (Impact of Computing). You've already started thinking like a computer scientist.

Discussion question

Should platforms be required to explain why they recommended something to you? Some countries are starting to require this. What would change if every recommendation came with a one-sentence explanation?
