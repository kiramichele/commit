The AP CSP exam happens in May. That feels far away right now, but everything you learn this year is building toward it — and the students who do well aren't necessarily the ones who know the most code. They're the ones who understood what the exam was actually testing and prepared for it deliberately. This lesson puts you in that group.

Let's be direct about what this exam is: it's a two-part test that measures whether you can think about computing — not just do it. One part is a portfolio of your own work. The other is a set of multiple-choice questions. Both reward the same underlying skill: clear, precise reasoning about how programs, data, and systems work.

The score scale

What the scores actually mean

About 64% of students pass (score 3 or higher). The average score is around 2.9 — which means this exam has real teeth. Students who treat it seriously do significantly better than students who assume it'll be easy because it doesn't require memorizing syntax.

The counterintuitive truth

More students fail from overconfidence than underpreparation

AP CSP is often marketed as "the accessible CS exam." That's true at the 3 level — but getting a 4 or 5 requires genuine fluency with AP pseudocode, data analysis concepts, and the ability to trace unfamiliar programs. The students who underestimate it are exactly the ones who end up in the 2 range.

The multiple choice section

What the 70 questions actually test

The MCQ section isn't a trivia quiz. Most questions give you a program, a scenario, or a dataset and ask you to reason about it. The most important skill isn't memorizing vocabulary — it's being able to read something you've never seen before and figure out what it does.

Exam tip

The exam includes an AP CSP Reference Sheet you can access during the test. It shows the complete pseudocode syntax — every construct, every operator. You don't need to memorize the syntax. You do need to be able to use it quickly. There's a difference.

The Create Performance Task

The PT: 30% of your score, built by you

The Create PT is a program you design and build — in any programming language, on any topic you choose. You submit it with written responses explaining your work and a short video demonstrating it. College Board readers score it using a rubric with 6 specific rows.

This is genuinely exciting: it's the only AP exam component where you get to choose what to build. Students who pick something they actually care about write much better responses than students who pick the first thing that comes to mind.

The Create PT — 30% of your score, built by you

The Create PT rubric — interactive

Read the rubric now. Beat it in April.

The Create PT is scored on 6 rows worth a total of 6 points. Each row is either earned (1 point) or not earned (0 points) — there's no partial credit. Below, you can read each rubric row alongside a sample student response. Your job: decide whether the response earns the point before you see the verdict.

Row 1 of 6
Program Purpose & Function
1 point

The response must describe:

  • The overall purpose of the program
  • What the program does (its functionality)
  • The input and output of the program
Common trap
Describing what the program does (its features) without explaining why it exists (its purpose). These are different things. "My program lets users enter a quiz answer and get feedback" is function. "My program helps students self-check their knowledge before an exam" is purpose.
Sample student response Point earned

The purpose of my program is to help students practice vocabulary before a test without needing a teacher or flashcards. The program presents one word at a time, accepts a definition from the user, and tells them whether they were correct or not. It takes a list of vocabulary words and definitions as input, and outputs a score at the end showing how many they got right.

Why this earns the point

It covers all three required elements: purpose (help students practice vocab independently), function (presents words, checks answers), and input/output (list of words/definitions in, score out). Nothing is missing. The language is clear and specific.

Row 2 of 6
Data Abstraction
1 point

The response must include a code segment showing:

  • A list (or other collection type) being used to store data
  • The list being used to fulfill the program's purpose

The written response must explain what the list represents and why using a list (rather than individual variables) manages complexity.

Common trap
Showing a list that stores data but never explaining why a list was necessary. The rubric specifically wants you to address how the list manages complexity — saying "I used a list to store the words" is not enough.
Sample student response Point not earned

I used a list called vocab_words to store all the vocabulary words in my program. The list contains strings, and I loop through it to show each word to the user. This made it easy to add more words later.

Why this misses the point

It describes the list and mentions a benefit, but doesn't explain how using a list manages complexity. A stronger response would say: "Without a list, I would need a separate variable for each word and would have to write the same quiz logic dozens of times. The list lets me write the loop once and handle any number of words with the same code."

Row 3 of 6
Managing Complexity
1 point

The response must include a code segment and explain how the list used in Row 2 manages complexity by:

  • Making the program easier to develop or maintain
  • Showing what would be harder or impossible without the list

This row is directly connected to Row 2 — same list, deeper explanation.

Common trap
Writing a generic statement like "lists make programs simpler." You need to connect it specifically to your program. How many variables would you have needed? What code would have been duplicated? Be concrete.
Sample student response Point earned

My quiz currently has 20 vocabulary words. Without the vocab_words list, I would need 20 separate variables (word1, word2, etc.) and 20 copies of the code that presents the word and checks the answer. If I wanted to add a 21st word, I'd have to write a 21st block of code. With the list, I write the loop once and adding new words requires only one extra line — a new entry in the list. The program handles 5 words or 500 words with the exact same logic.

Why this earns the point

Concrete and specific — gives the exact number of variables needed, explains what duplication would look like, and describes what would change when scaling up. This is exactly the level of specificity the rubric wants.

Row 4 of 6
Procedural Abstraction
1 point

The response must include a code segment showing a student-defined procedure (function) that:

  • Has a name, parameter(s), and return value or side effect
  • Is called somewhere in the program

The written response must describe what the procedure does and how it contributes to the program's overall functionality.

Common trap
Using a procedure with no parameters — for example def run_quiz(): with no arguments. The rubric specifically requires at least one parameter. A function that takes nothing in and produces something is not demonstrating abstraction.
Sample student response Point not earned

I wrote a function called check_answer that checks whether the user's response is correct. It's called in the main loop each time the user submits an answer. The function compares the input to the correct definition and returns True or False.

Why this misses the point

The written response is solid, but without seeing the code, we can't confirm the function has a parameter. If the student wrote def check_answer(): and accessed global variables inside, this row would not be earned. Always show the full function definition in the code segment — the reader needs to see the parameter explicitly.

Row 5 of 6
Algorithm Implementation
1 point

The response must include a code segment showing an algorithm that includes all three of:

  • Sequencing — steps executed in order
  • Selection — a conditional (if/else)
  • Iteration — a loop

The written response must explain how the algorithm works in enough detail that someone could reproduce it without seeing the code.

Common trap
Writing a shallow description like "the loop goes through each word and checks if the answer is right." The rubric wants detail: what happens first, what the condition checks for, what happens in each branch, when the loop ends. Think of it as explaining your code to someone who can't see it.
Sample student response Point earned

The algorithm first initializes a score counter to 0. It then iterates through each word in the vocab_words list using a for loop. For each word, it displays the word and prompts the user to enter a definition. It then checks whether the user's input matches the stored correct definition (after converting both to lowercase). If they match, it increments the score counter and prints "Correct!" If they don't match, it prints the correct definition. After the loop completes, it displays the final score out of the total number of words.

Why this earns the point

Hits all three: sequencing (initializes counter → iterates → checks → displays), selection (if match / else), and iteration (for loop). The description is detailed enough to reproduce the algorithm without the code. That's exactly the bar.

Row 6 of 6
Testing
1 point

The response must describe two calls to the student-defined procedure from Row 4, with:

  • Different arguments/conditions for each call
  • The expected result for each call
  • An explanation of what was being tested
Common trap
Describing two calls that test the same thing — for example, two correct answers or two wrong answers. The two calls must test meaningfully different conditions: one should cover the "true" branch, one the "false" branch, or one should test edge cases like an empty string or unexpected capitalization.
Sample student response Point earned

I tested check_answer("photosynthesis", "the process by which plants make food using sunlight") with the correct definition. I expected it to return True, and it did. Then I tested check_answer("photosynthesis", "when plants grow") with an incorrect definition. I expected it to return False. This tested that the function correctly handles both a matching and a non-matching input, which are the two conditions the program depends on to score the quiz.

Why this earns the point

Two calls, different arguments, different expected results, clear explanation of what's being tested (the two branches of the condition). Textbook. The function name from Row 4 is used consistently, which also shows these rows are coherent — readers appreciate when the PT tells a consistent story.

Row 1 of 6
The key insight

The rubric rows tell a single story

Notice how Rows 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 all connect to each other — the same list from Row 2 appears in Row 3, the same function from Row 4 is called in Row 6. The highest-scoring PTs are coherent: they describe one well-designed program consistently. A PT that jumps between unrelated examples for different rows raises red flags for readers.

Exam strategy

How to actually prepare — and what not to waste time on

The AP pseudocode questions are the ones students most reliably underestimate. They're not hard — but they require practice with a specific notation you've never seen before.

— Pattern across years of AP CSP results

Here's what the data says about what moves scores:

Fig. 2 The most effective AP CSP preparation is active — tracing code, translating pseudocode, and explaining concepts out loud — not passive re-reading of notes.

High-leverage preparation:

  • Practice reading and tracing AP pseudocode — weekly, starting now
  • Do past AP MCQs under timed conditions — not open-book
  • For every wrong answer, read the explanation for all four choices
  • Build your Create PT on something you actually care about
  • Write your PT responses as if the reader has never seen your code

Lower-leverage (but people think it helps):

  • Memorizing vocabulary definitions without using them in context
  • Re-reading your notes the night before
  • Picking a PT topic that "sounds impressive" rather than one you understand
  • Waiting until March to think about the PT
