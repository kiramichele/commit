You are a data broker

Your job is to build profiles on people and sell them to advertisers, insurers, and employers. You have no name, no photo, no direct contact with your subject — just data points collected from apps, loyalty programs, public records, and purchase histories. Let's see how much you can figure out.

The ambiguity is the lesson

Profile B is intentionally less clear — but a real data broker wouldn't stop at 5 data points. With 500, most of the ambiguity disappears. The next phase shows you what that looks like.

What just happened

Two separate people — no names, no photos, no direct contact — became one merged profile. Data brokers routinely combine records from multiple sources to build a more complete picture. You never consented to being in this database. You never knew it existed. It was assembled from the side effects of your daily life.

Phase 3 of 4
What you just did

You were the algorithm

You just did manually what a data broker's software does automatically — and much faster, with far more data. The inferences you made from 5 data points are the same inferences a model makes from 500, except the model never second-guesses itself and never feels uncomfortable about it.

Question 1
When you were building the inferences, did it feel like an invasion of privacy — or just like solving a puzzle? Why do you think it felt that way?
Question 2
None of the data in Profile A or B was stolen. All of it was legally collected. Does "legal" mean the same thing as "okay"? Where would you draw the line?
Question 3
The broker profile flags "stress indicators" and sells that to credit lenders. Should a lender be allowed to use your app usage and late-night food orders to make a lending decision? Why or why not?
Question 4
What would you change about how data is collected and sold — if you could change anything? Be specific: what rule, law, or technical requirement would you propose?
AP connection

Everything in this activity maps to AP standard IOC-2.A (data privacy and the impact of data collection) and IOC-2.B (the implications of access to information). The written response questions above are the kind of thing the AP exam asks in its open-ended sections — practice articulating a position and defending it.

Activity complete.

You built two profiles from anonymous data, saw what 500 data points look like at scale, and thought through the ethics of a system most people don't know exists.

That's the foundation for everything Unit 5 covers on AI, bias, and the impact of computing.
